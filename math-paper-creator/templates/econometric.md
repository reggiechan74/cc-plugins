## Outline

### Introduction
The economic question, policy context, and why econometric methods
are needed to identify causal effects or structural parameters.
**Symbols:** none

### Economic Model
The theoretical framework generating the estimating equations.
Behavioral assumptions, market structure, or equilibrium conditions.
**Symbols:** Set, Parameter

### Data Structure
Panel dimensions, cross-section units, time periods, and variable
definitions. The index sets describing the dataset.
**Symbols:** Set, Parameter

### Identification Strategy
How endogeneity is addressed — instruments, natural experiments,
exclusion restrictions, or structural assumptions that enable
consistent estimation.
**Symbols:** Variable, Constraint

### Estimation Procedure
The estimator — OLS, IV/2SLS, GMM, MLE, or control function.
Moment conditions, weighting matrices, and asymptotic properties.
**Symbols:** Expression, Objective

### Specification Tests
Diagnostics validating the identification assumptions — overidentification
tests, weak instrument checks, Hausman tests, or placebo regressions.
**Symbols:** Constraint, Expression

### Results Interpretation
Coefficient estimates, standard errors, economic magnitudes, and
policy implications.
**Symbols:** Expression

### Robustness Checks (optional)
Alternative specifications, subsamples, different instruments, or
sensitivity to functional form assumptions.
**Symbols:** Expression, Parameter

### Conclusion
Summary of findings, identification caveats, external validity,
and policy recommendations.
**Symbols:** none
